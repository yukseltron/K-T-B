The Visitors

They arrived with eager and naive faces into our small town. A playful demeanour masking their mischief, they all decide to stay the night. The makeshift motel at the bar was the only way to accommodate the six young strangers. I told them it'd be easier to move on to the next city. They refused but willingly paid whatever large cost the barkeep proposed.

"This is where we want to be. No one knows us here."

They came as tourists, but we are not a tourist town. There is no statue, no museum, no landmark worth seeing or still standing. Few want to stay here and even fewer remain each passing year. So I kept an eye on the visitors. I was firm, welcoming, but unfriendly. Yet they were not deterred.

By next morning, they were gone. Other than them having been a bit loud the night before, there was no damage done. Nothing was out of the ordinary.

Then, the detective came from far away. He asked for the barkeep and arrested the man. He then arrested me too. I was furious because I knew we had done nothing wrong. They wouldn't listen and we wouldn't win in court. The barkeep and I were interned for twenty years each. He would die after eight and pass on his bar to me. Two years later, after giving a fake apology to ten different parents, I was given parole. Ten years later, I returned to the only place I knew.

I had lost my old job, and the bar had deteriorated beyond what I could afford to fix. I use what's left of it as my home. But the old signage and decrepit decor remain. Today, eight young visitors arrived asking for a place to stay.

"We've never heard of here before. We'll pay any price."
